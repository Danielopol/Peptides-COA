How to Read a Peptide COA (Certificate of Analysis) for Beginners
You keep seeing "third-party tested" and "COA available" on vendor sites. But do you actually know what you're looking at when you open one?

Most beginners don't. They see a document with numbers and a logo and assume it's legit. That's how people get burned.

A COA is your only proof that what's in the vial matches what's on the label. Here's how to actually read one.

What Is a COA?

Certificate of Analysis. It's a lab report from an independent testing facility that verifies the peptide's identity and purity. The vendor sends a sample to a lab, the lab runs tests, and the lab produces this document.

The key word is independent. The vendor shouldn't be testing their own products. That's like grading your own exam.

The 4 Things You Need to Check

1. Purity Percentage

This is the big number everyone looks for. It tells you what percentage of the sample is actually the peptide you ordered versus impurities or degraded material.

What you want: 98% or higher for most peptides. 99%+ is excellent.

What to avoid: Anything below 95% is questionable. If there's no purity listed at all, walk away.

Some COAs show this as "HPLC Purity" which refers to the testing method used. High-performance liquid chromatography is the industry standard for peptide purity testing.

2. Peptide Identity Confirmation

The COA should confirm the peptide is actually what it claims to be. This is usually done through mass spectrometry (MS) testing.

Look for: The molecular weight matching the expected value for that peptide. For BPC-157, the molecular weight should be around 1419. For TB-500, around 4963.

If the molecular weight is way off or missing entirely, you might not be getting what you paid for.

3. Batch or Lot Number

Every COA should reference a specific batch number. This connects the test results to a specific production run.

Why it matters: If you order a peptide and the batch number on your vial doesn't match the COA on the website, that test result might not apply to what you received.

Legit vendors will provide the COA for the exact batch you're buying, not just a generic report from six months ago.

4. Lab Name and Test Date

Check who actually ran the tests and when.

Look for: A named third-party laboratory. Some reputable ones you might see include Janoshik, Colmaric Analyticals, or other established analytical labs.

The test date matters too. A COA from two years ago doesn't tell you anything about the peptide sitting in their inventory today. Recent testing within the last few months is ideal.

Red Flags That Should Make You Walk Away

No lab name listed. If they won't tell you who tested it, assume nobody did.

Blurry or low-resolution documents. Legit COAs are clear and readable. Blurry images are often edited or fake.

Purity conveniently at exactly 99.9% for everything. Real lab results have variation. If every single product shows perfect numbers, someone might be fabricating documents.

No batch number or a batch number that doesn't match your product.

The vendor claims they do their own testing. In-house testing means nothing. Third-party verification is the whole point.

How to Actually Use This

Before you order from any vendor, find their COAs. Most legit vendors post them publicly or send them on request.

Open the COA for the specific peptide you want. Check those four things: purity percentage, identity confirmation, batch number, and lab name with date.

If anything is missing or looks off, ask the vendor directly. Their response tells you a lot. Legit vendors answer quickly and transparently. Sketchy ones get defensive or dodge the question.

The Bottom Line

A COA doesn't guarantee a vendor is perfect. But the absence of one, or a sketchy one, guarantees you should look elsewhere.

Five minutes of checking can save you from injecting mystery powder.
